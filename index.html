How it works

We have put a lot of thought into how we approached this problem and how we designed our device, what features to include, and the impact this device will have on the communities where it is installed. Using our years of experience in developing countries installing and repairing handpumps, we believe we have come up with a solution that is technologically appropriate and can help solve the issue of broken handpumps once and for all.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is this important?

With as many as 30% of all handpumps broken at any given time, there are hundreds of millions of people who used to have access to clean water but are now just out of reach of it because of broken handpumps. This is beyond the 800 million people that still have never had access to clean water.

What sets your device apart from what other groups are developing?

We have included many features that others lack. Our device collects multiple data points including flow, handle strokes, time, and location. Most other devices only collect data on flow. By collecting different data points, we can compare items against each other to pull out hidden insights that will give us a greater depth of knowledge into how the pump is functioning. In addition, the other device options rely on local infrastructure and require the installer to replace batteries once or twice per year. In contrast, our device is completely untethers itself from local infrastructure by generating its own power and sending data via satellite.

Isn't sending data via satellite really expensive?

While sending data is more expensive than using cellular, the cost difference reasonable and the added benefits of satellite make it a clear winner. Handpumps are used primarily in developing countries where infrastructure is poor and unreliable. Our research has found that even more developed countries in Africa experience frequent outages and data losses. Another frequent problem with cellular data is that it can be turned off by the government. Satellite transmissions are immune to such government oversight and tampering. Lastly, setup and maintenance is a much simpler task with satellite data. Instead of different SIM cards, data costs, and contracts for every single country, Re:Water customers can enjoy one global contract and a centralized payment and subscription system.

Why is Re:Water a company instead of a non-profit?

Non-profits serve end-users who cannot afford their services. For example, most third-world communities cannot afford the high cost of drilling a water well. Re:Water's end-user is the organizations and non-profits that are stakeholders of the water well. These groups can afford our services. In addition, Re:Water wants to attract the best investors, mentors, and team members as possible. In order to lure the best people, Re:Water needs to be able to pay competitive salaries. We're not doing this to get rich, but instead to develop the best product possible for our customers. Most of the money made will be reinvested into the company so that we can continue to develop solutions to water challenges around the world.

What keeps people from just stealing your device off the pump?

While nothing in the developing world is theft proof, we have taken special care to make the Re:Water device as tough and theft resistant as possible. Besides being constructed out of solid steel, we also use proprietary Bryce fasteners that require a specific bit to be removed. Additionally, we have protected the solar panel against theft and vandalism by putting it behind 6mm of hardened clear acrylic. This is the same stuff bullet-proof glass is made out of and is over 30 time more shatter resistant than glass.

Why don't organizations and non-profits just followup on past projects instead?

Organizations have struggled with this issue for a long time. While most organizations would like to physically visit past projects on a regular basis they have found it to be prohibitively expensive. Our experience and research has found that just to visit past projects one to three times per year can cost a organization $40-$80 per month per well depending on conditions and country of operation. Even at this high cost, the organization only knows if a pump is functioning or not up to the day they visited. The well could break down the next day and they may not know until they visited again 4-12 months later.

Story behind Re:Water

In 2008, our founder and CEO Stephen Huber travelled to war-torn South Sudan with nothing more than a backpack and vision to create a new water well drilling organization. With the support of a private foundation in Texas and local church leaders, Stephen developed from the ground up a thriving organization that has now drilled almost 500 water wells in East Africa that now provides clean water to more than 200,000 people

Very early into his experience, Stephen saw first hand the need for a solution to the problem of broken wells. South Sudan is one of the most difficult countries in the world with terrible roads, high levels of insecurity, and barely any infrastructure. He realized that whatever the solution, it needed to be tough, collect the right data, and be able to work and transmit data from anywhere without relying on local infrastructure.

As the years progressed, Stephen continued to plan his device and found more areas in the water sector where appropriate technology could help. The culmination of these insights is the Re:Water remote monitoring system.

After six years working in Africa, Stephen completed his mandate and moved with his wife and African dog back to the United States in late 2014. Since then, Stephen has been pursuing and developing and testing the Re:Water system. His biggest accomplishment thus far has been developing a working prototype that was installed on a well in South Sudan. Within minutes of turning on the device, data from a well in remote South Sudan was viewable from anywhere in the world! Unfortunately, after three weeks of reliably sending data, the device experience a power failure. The issue that caused the failure has since been fixed and a new and improved prototype is undergoing automated testing in the US.

Infrastructure Independent

The Re:Water team has experience working in some of the most under developed countries in the world. From this experience we have learned that it is a mistake to be dependent on unstable local infrastructure. Beyond that, we have a self-imposed requirement that our device needs to work in areas that have no infrastructure at all!

The Re:Water device is the first to be completely independent of local infrastructure by using satellite communications and generating it's own power. Our goal get the data needed to keep clean water flowing while reducing the costs non-profits and organizations spend visiting past projects or changing batteries in sensors. To accomplish this, we are building a device that can last at least five years maintenance free.

Accountability

Unless you have worked in a area for a long time, it is difficult to know who will give you the best return on your investment. Drilling new water wells is expensive and we want you to know who you're entrusting your money to. Above everything else, Re:Water desires that communities continue to receive access to clean water. The best way to do this is to ensure that operators and organizations are held accountable for the quality of their work. A little healthy competition is never a bad thing and in this case, the villages and communities served by these operators stand to benefit the most.

Our ratings system is not reliant on reviews along, but our algorithm also uses real data from pumps and compares their repair and failure rates against others in the same area.

Some of the features of the reviews and rating system includes:

  • Operators Ranking System
  • Compares user reviews with real data
  • Easily compare operator services
  • View operator profiles and easily connect

Map Handpumps

Organizations and operators spend a ton of money on area surveys when planning to drill wells. One reason they do this is because most developing countries do not have a good system of tracking new wells installed. Re:Water wants to change this by including a GPS chip in every device. While this increases the cost of the device by about $20, the added benefit of receiving updated and accurate coordinates of every pump with our device installed has a potential to solve this problem plaguing organizations and governments.

Imagine for a minute if in a country every water well had the Re:Water system installed. Organizations could know instantly where every well has been drilled and which areas were still in need without spending any money on surveys. In addition, drillers would have access to drilling logs of other wells in the area including hydrogeological data that could save thousands of dollars in wasted money due to drilling a dry well. Lastly, the government would have a national rural water grid at their fingertips allowing them to direct resources where needed.

Some of the features of the maps feature includes:

  • Know where wells are and are not instaneously
  • Visual summary of well functional status
  • Quickly check area water saturation

Actionable Data

Having more data and information is never a bad thing but data that can be used to make correct decisions can be priceless. Re:Water is using complex algorithms to pull out the data you and your organization need to make intelligent decisions. Doing so increases your impact and reduces costs.

Because our device collects multiple data points, we can do much more than just tell you if a well is working or not. In fact, with our data we will be able to predict pump failure ahead of time ensuring clean water never stops flowing. Even though we only collect three more data points than other devices, this allows use to do exponentially more analysis'. And since we are collecting the same data on potentially thousands of pumps, we are creating the best field-based longevity and durability test data that has previously been impossible to collect.

Global Coverage

This feature is one of those things that sets us far apart from other devices. While we have been questioned and criticized for using "more expensive" satellite instead of cellular GSM, tt is something we feel so strongly about that we are unwilling to compromise. Why do we feel this way?

We have spent years on the ground and have experienced the unreliability and setup hurtles of GSM firsthand. With satellite, all devices are managed centrally, a connection is always available, and they are immune to government tampering. With recent technology advances, our devices receive sent confirmation so we can be sure our data comes through.

As for the issue of pricing, we have negotiated contracts with satellite reseller and have received pricing far below market rates. This reseller understand the benefits of the Re:Water device will have on society and has agreed to give us a special price that competes with the cost of cellular.

This means that you can save countless lives and ensure clean water access for as many as 500 people for the cost of a Netflix subscription.
